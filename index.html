Research

The power grid was not built with data centers like today's in mind. Driven by AI, a single new facility can draw hundreds of megawatts, comparable to a small city, and many are being planned faster than transmission lines can be built to serve them. My work asks a simple question: instead of treating these loads as a fixed burden, can we take advantage of the fact that computational work is, in many ways, flexible, and use that flexibility to help the grid run better?

A lot of computing tasks do not strictly need to happen at a specific time, or in a specific place. AI training jobs can often be paused, or shifted between data centers in different regions. I have been studying what happens if the grid operator's day-ahead unit commitment model is allowed to plan around this flexibility, both in time and across locations. In our test cases on the IEEE 24- and 73-bus systems, this can meaningfully relieve transmission congestion and reduce the amount of wind and solar energy that has to be curtailed.

A related question is one of planning rather than operation: if we know large data centers are coming, where can the existing grid actually host them, and what new transmission or generation needs to be built? I have been working on hosting capacity analysis under progressively higher-fidelity security models (from OPF through N-1 SCUC), finding that omitting N-1 contingency constraints can overestimate bus-level hosting capacity by roughly 20%. On the expansion planning side, our co-optimized generation-and-transmission model with embedded unit commitment shows that data center load can fundamentally reshape optimal investment decisions, in some cases substituting strategically sited generation for transmission reinforcement entirely.
